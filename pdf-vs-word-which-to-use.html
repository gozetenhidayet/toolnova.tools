PDF vs Word: Which Format Should You Use?

5 min read · Updated July 2026

Both formats can hold the same content, but they behave very differently once they leave your computer. Picking the right one depends on one question: do you want the recipient to edit it, or just read it exactly as you designed it?

The core difference

PDFWord (.docx)
LayoutFixed — looks identical everywhereCan shift depending on fonts/software installed
EditingDifficult by designEasy, that's the point
File sizeUsually smaller for text-heavy docsCan vary widely
Best forFinal, "done" documentsDocuments still in progress

When to use PDF

  • Resumes and CVs — you want it to look exactly the same on the hiring manager's screen as it does on yours, regardless of what software they open it with.
  • Contracts and signed documents — PDFs resist accidental edits and are the standard for anything that needs a signature.
  • Anything you're printing — layout, margins, and page breaks stay exactly where you put them.
  • Sharing with people who may not have Word — PDFs open in any browser, no software required.

When to use Word

  • Collaborative documents — if someone needs to add comments, track changes, or rewrite sections.
  • Templates — anything meant to be reused and modified by different people.
  • Drafts — while a document is still being written, Word's editing tools make revisions much faster than editing a PDF.

You don't have to choose forever

A common workflow: write and edit in Word while the document is a work in progress, then convert to PDF once it's final and ready to send. If someone sends you a PDF that you actually need to edit — say, a form or an old document — you can convert it back to Word, make your changes, and either keep it as a Word file or re-export to PDF when you're done.
