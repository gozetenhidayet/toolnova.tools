What actually makes a password strong

Password advice has shifted over the years — first it was "use special characters," then "use a completely random string," now it's often "use a long passphrase." The underlying principle hasn't really changed: the two things that matter most are length and unpredictability, in that order.

A long, randomly generated password is effectively impossible to guess or brute-force with current technology — but it's also impossible to memorize, which is fine, because you shouldn't be memorizing it. The realistic modern approach is to use a password manager to store a unique, random password for every account, and only memorize a small handful of master passwords for things like your device lock screen.

The single biggest real-world risk isn't a weak individual password — it's reusing the same password across multiple sites. When one service gets breached, reused passwords let attackers walk straight into your other accounts. A unique, generated password per account closes that door entirely.

Are these passwords actually random?

Yes — the generator uses your browser's cryptographically secure random number source, not a predictable pattern, so each password is unique and not guessable from previous ones.

Is anything I generate sent to a server?

No — passwords are generated entirely in your browser and are never transmitted, logged, or stored anywhere.

Should I memorize my generated password?

For most accounts, no — use a password manager to store unique, randomly generated passwords for every site, and only memorize a small handful of master passwords.

Is a longer password always better than one with more symbols?

Length matters more than symbol variety for resisting brute-force guessing. A long random password with just letters and numbers is generally stronger than a short one packed with symbols.

How long should my password be?

For most modern accounts, 16 characters or more is a reasonable minimum; some security-sensitive accounts benefit from 20+ characters.

Can I reuse the same strong password across multiple sites?

No — this is one of the biggest real-world security risks. If one service is breached, a reused password lets attackers access your other accounts too. Generate a unique password per account.
